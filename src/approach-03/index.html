Approach 03

Chromatic Dimension Tokens

Most design systems name token dimensions after surface roles: background, text, border. This approach inverts that logic — dimensions are named after perceptual qualities of the color itself, borrowed from color science and typographic vocabulary.

Token Architecture

Token Surface role Origin & intent
--chroma-* vivid, saturated fill From OKLCH color space (lightness, chroma, hue). The full-strength expression of a role.
--echo-* faint tinted background The faint reverberation of that hue on a near-neutral field.
--trace-* border / edge line A thin line that marks a boundary without filling the space.
--mark-* ink on a vivid surface Typographic: ink stamped onto a chroma surface. Must contrast against --chroma-*.
--script-* ink on a soft/transparent surface Typographic: ink written on a pale or transparent surface. Readable against --echo-*.
--chroma-primary: #9B7B2E → --b-chroma: var(--chroma-primary) → background-color: var(--b-chroma)
--echo-success: #EAF2EC → --b-echo: var(--echo-success) → background-color: var(--b-echo)
--trace-evening: #7B6A9B → --b-trace: var(--trace-evening) → border-color: var(--b-trace)
--mark-primary: #FDFAF2 → --b-mark: var(--mark-primary) → color: var(--b-mark)
--script-error: #6A3324 → --b-script: var(--script-error) → color: var(--b-script)

Interaction States

Hover, active, and disabled states are handled entirely with filter: brightness() and opacity on the component — no per-state color tokens are defined. This keeps the token layer focused on identity (what a role is) rather than behaviour (what it does when pressed). The trade-off: interaction states cannot be individually themed or overridden via tokens, but the token surface stays minimal — 5 dimensions per role, nothing more.
